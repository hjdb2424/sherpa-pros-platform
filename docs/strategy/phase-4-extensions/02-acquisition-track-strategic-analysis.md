# Strategic Analysis — Acquisition Pathways

## Purpose

This document maps the realistic strategic-acquirer set for Sherpa Pros across Phase 4 (Months 18-36) and Phase 5 (Months 36-60), the comparable transactions, what each acquirer wants from Sherpa, what Sherpa loses, and the pre-engagement playbook that keeps optionality alive without committing the company to any single outcome. It also frames the Initial Public Offering (IPO) alternative.

The thesis is simple: Sherpa Pros is built to operate as an independent compounding platform. We do not optimize for a sale. We optimize for compounding economics, and a sale becomes possible — at attractive multiples — because we are valuable to a small set of strategic acquirers who would rather buy than build. This document is how we keep that optionality intact without spending founder energy chasing it.

## 1. Acquirer-by-acquirer analysis

### 1.1 Home Depot (NYSE: HD)

**Why this is the leading candidate.** Home Depot's Pro segment is its single most strategic growth lever. The Pro Desk and the Home Depot Pro X Pro account ecosystem already represent ~50% of Home Depot total revenue. Home Depot is acquisitive when a target lets them reach more pros faster than they could organically: the 2015 Interline Brands acquisition ($1.6B) bought them maintenance-repair-operations distribution, the 2020 HD Supply re-acquisition ($8B) re-bought them industrial distribution. A Sherpa Pros acquisition extends the Pro relationship from "pro buys materials at Home Depot" to "pro gets jobs from Home Depot, completes them, and buys materials from Home Depot," which is the holy grail of foot-traffic synergy.

**What Home Depot wants from Sherpa Pros.**
- **Foot-traffic synergy.** Every Sherpa pro becomes a Home Depot Pro account holder; every job completed drives materials demand. Home Depot can quantify the lift in stores within 25 miles of any Sherpa Hub.
- **Pro loyalty.** Sherpa Score is a richer pro-loyalty signal than Home Depot's internal Pro X Pro account data. Home Depot has been searching for a structured pro-trust signal for a decade.
- **Materials supply chain co-investment.** Sherpa Materials engine routes pros to whichever supplier has the right product at the right price. Home Depot would want to reroute that traffic toward Home Depot stores. (This is also where Sherpa loses something — see below.)
- **Vertical integration thesis.** Home Depot is the canonical four-layer-integrated retailer. Sherpa is the canonical four-layer-integrated marketplace. The strategic logic writes itself.

**Comparable transactions.**
- Interline Brands acquisition by Home Depot (2015): $1.6B. Maintenance-repair-operations distribution. Comparable pro-distribution thesis.
- HD Supply re-acquisition by Home Depot (2020): $8B. Industrial distribution rollback. Larger, less directly comparable.
- Compex Legal Services acquisition by Home Depot (rumored, never closed): would have extended Home Depot into pro-services. Demonstrates Home Depot's interest in services adjacencies.

**Estimated valuation envelope.** $400M-$1.0B+ at the Phase 5 entry point (Series C close Annual Recurring Revenue $60M-$100M, growth >50% Year-over-Year), depending on strategic premium. Could stretch to $1.5B if Home Depot views Sherpa as the way to win the $540B residential trade-materials market vs Lowe's.

**What Sherpa loses in a Home Depot acquisition.**
- **Independence.** Roadmap subordinated to Home Depot Pro Desk strategy. International expansion may decelerate (Home Depot's international footprint is smaller).
- **Brand purity.** "Sherpa Pros, a Home Depot company" is an option, but more likely Home Depot consolidates the brand under Pro X Pro account or Pro Desk over 24-36 months.
- **Materials engine neutrality.** Sherpa Materials engine routes pros to optimal supplier today; under Home Depot ownership, the engine would be biased toward Home Depot. This compromises pro trust — the very thing that drove the acquisition value.
- **Multi-product roadmap pressure.** Embedded fintech (Sherpa Lending, Sherpa Insurance) and Software-as-a-Service spinouts (Sherpa codes engine Application Programming Interface) likely get deprioritized; Home Depot is a retailer, not a financial services or Software-as-a-Service company.

**Founder calculus.** A Home Depot acquisition at $600M-$1B in Phase 5 Year 1 returns the cap table at a 60-100x multiple on the Phase 0 Wefunder round, ~10-15x for Series A, ~6-10x for Series B, ~2-4x for Series C. Founder ownership at that point ~12-15% post-Series C dilution. Founder net at $600M = $72M-$90M before tax. Founder net at $1B = $120M-$150M before tax. Strong outcome but founder loses operational independence.

### 1.2 Lowe's (NYSE: LOW)

**Why this is a strategic stretch.** Lowe's pro segment is roughly half the size of Home Depot's pro segment and historically less acquisitive in pro tech. Lowe's has shown interest in pro through the Lowe's For Pros program but has not made a transformative acquisition in this space. A Lowe's acquisition would be a "we cannot afford to let Home Depot buy this" defensive play more than a strategic-fit play.

**What Lowe's would want from Sherpa Pros.** Same logic as Home Depot — pro loyalty, foot traffic, materials throughput — but with weaker conviction and a smaller installed base of pros to convert.

**Comparable transactions.** Lowe's has not made a comparable pro-services acquisition in the recent decade. The closest was the 2018 acquisition of Maintenance Supply Headquarters (~$500M, multifamily maintenance distribution), which is more property-management adjacent than residential-trade adjacent.

**Estimated valuation envelope.** Likely $300M-$700M, lower than Home Depot because Lowe's strategic rationale is weaker and Lowe's would less likely engage in a competitive bidding process unless Home Depot moves first. More plausibly an acqui-hire territory unless Lowe's decides to accelerate pro-vertical investment.

**What Sherpa loses.** Same losses as Home Depot acquisition but with lower probability of generous earn-out terms.

**Founder calculus.** Lowe's is the secondary-bidder option that creates pricing tension if Home Depot is the primary. Not a primary outreach target.

### 1.3 Insurance carrier (Travelers, Chubb, Liberty Mutual)

**Why this is the dark-horse candidate.** Insurance carriers are aggressively searching for embedded distribution channels — ways to attach insurance products to non-insurance transactions where the buyer is already in a buying mode. Sherpa Flex's per-project $1M platform liability tier is exactly this thesis: the pro is buying access to platform jobs and the insurance comes along automatically. An insurance carrier acquiring Sherpa Pros gets:

- **Distribution into the trade economy.** Every job sold through Sherpa Marketplace is a moment to attach service-warranty insurance, completion-guarantee insurance, or commercial general liability cross-sell.
- **Underwriting data.** Sherpa Score is a proprietary pro-trust signal that no insurance carrier has access to. Underwriting commercial general liability for trade pros gets much better with that data.
- **The Sherpa Flex precedent.** Sherpa Flex tier is already a distribution channel for per-project liability insurance. An insurance carrier would scale this from $5K-job pilots to all jobs, all tiers.

**What an insurance carrier wants.** Distribution. Underwriting data. Cross-sell. Embedded-insurance product proof-of-concept that they can replicate across other vertical marketplaces.

**Comparable transactions.**
- Hippo (NYSE: HIPO) — Initial Public Offering 2021 at $5B (since collapsed to ~$300M). Insurtech as standalone — direct comparison difficult.
- Lemonade (NYSE: LMND) — Initial Public Offering 2020 at $4B (current ~$1.5B). Direct-to-consumer insurance, weak comparable.
- Embedded-insurance acquisitions: Travelers acquisition of Trōv Inc. (insurtech infrastructure, ~$200M, undisclosed). Chubb acquisition of Healthy Paws Pet Insurance (~$200M-$300M). Pattern: carriers pay 4-6x revenue for embedded distribution.

**Estimated valuation envelope.** $400M-$900M, depending on how much insurance premium volume the carrier can underwrite through Sherpa once they own it. The valuation includes both the Sherpa Pros standalone value AND the present value of insurance premium that flows through the channel post-acquisition. Travelers or Chubb could pay a premium because Sherpa's insurance-distribution leverage exceeds Sherpa's direct profit-and-loss.

**What Sherpa loses.** Less than under a Home Depot acquisition. Insurance carriers typically operate acquisitions semi-independently (Travelers operates IndustryEdge and Simply Business as semi-autonomous units). Brand probably preserved. Roadmap probably preserved (carrier wants the engine to keep growing the channel, not to suppress it). The biggest loss is governance — carriers are heavily regulated, so every decision routes through compliance.

**Founder calculus.** This is the cleanest founder outcome. Strong valuation, semi-independent operations, brand and team preserved, embedded-fintech roadmap accelerated rather than killed.

### 1.4 Procore Technologies (NYSE: PCOR)

**Why this is a vertical-extension play.** Procore is the dominant commercial construction Project Management Software-as-a-Service player, currently ~$1.1B Annual Recurring Revenue. Procore's growth thesis is vertical-extension: into new construction segments (residential, light-commercial, infrastructure) and into new workflow surfaces (financials, materials, labor). Sherpa Pros is the residential + light-commercial labor + materials marketplace that Procore does not have and would have to build over 5+ years.

**What Procore wants.** Residential and light-commercial market entry without the multi-year build. Sherpa Marketplace + Sherpa Hub + Sherpa codes engine + Sherpa Materials engine = an entire vertical extension out of the box.

**Comparable transactions.** Procore historically has been organic-growth-focused. Comparable acquisitions are smaller tuck-ins (LaborChart $200M, Esticom $100M-tier). Procore has not made a transformative billion-dollar acquisition. A Sherpa acquisition would be Procore's largest deal.

**Estimated valuation envelope.** $500M-$900M. Procore's revenue multiple is ~10-12x trailing-twelve-month Annual Recurring Revenue (as of 2026-Q1). Sherpa at $60M-$100M Annual Recurring Revenue at Phase 5 entry would price at $600M-$1.2B on Procore's own multiple.

**What Sherpa loses.** Independent brand probably loses (Procore consolidates under "Procore Residential" or similar). Marketplace economics potentially get re-architected toward Software as a Service seat licenses (Procore's revenue model) — this could compromise the take-rate flywheel. Pros pay for software, marketplace economics suppressed.

**Founder calculus.** Lower-probability acquirer than Home Depot or insurance carrier. Procore would have to develop Mergers and Acquisitions appetite they do not currently demonstrate. Would price at fair-multiple (no strategic premium).

### 1.5 Reverse direction — Sherpa acquires BuilderTrend / JobNimbus / Smaller residential Property Management Software-as-a-Service

**Why this is the most likely Mergers and Acquisitions pattern in Phase 5.** BuilderTrend (~$60M-$100M Annual Recurring Revenue, Bridgeport Capital portfolio), JobNimbus (~$30M-$50M Annual Recurring Revenue), CoConstruct (acquired by BuilderTrend 2021, ~$12M Annual Recurring Revenue when acquired) are all residential Property Management Software-as-a-Service tools serving general contractors and remodelers. They have customer relationships Sherpa wants. They lack the marketplace, the materials engine, the codes engine, the Sherpa Hub network. Sherpa at $60M-$100M Annual Recurring Revenue with $40M-$80M Series C cash on the balance sheet has the capacity to acquire these.

**Strategic logic.** Acquire BuilderTrend or JobNimbus → get 30K+ general contractor + remodeler customers overnight → cross-sell Sherpa Marketplace as the labor + materials engine inside their existing Project Management workflow. This is the inverse of Procore acquiring Sherpa: Sherpa acquires the Software-as-a-Service tool and becomes its marketplace backbone.

**Estimated cost.** $80M-$300M depending on target. Funded from Series C acquisition war chest (~$12M-$20M earmarked, supplemented by stock-and-cash structure for larger targets).

**Phasing.** Phase 5 Year 1 (Months 36-48). Director of Corporate Development (new hire Phase 5 Month 0) leads diligence and integration.

### 1.6 Initial Public Offering (IPO) alternative pathway

**When IPO becomes the default path.** When (a) Sherpa Pros reaches $200M+ Annual Recurring Revenue with 25%+ Year-over-Year growth, (b) Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization is positive at $50M+, (c) operating history exceeds 5 years from incorporation, and (d) public-market multiples for vertical-Software-as-a-Service marketplaces are healthy. Target window: Phase 5 Year 4 to Year 5 (Months 60-84 from current date).

**Comparable IPOs.**
- Procore Initial Public Offering (May 2021): $9.6B opening valuation on $400M trailing-twelve-month Annual Recurring Revenue. 24x revenue multiple at peak. Currently trades at ~10x.
- Toast Initial Public Offering (September 2021): $20B opening valuation on $700M trailing-twelve-month Annual Recurring Revenue. 28x revenue multiple at peak. Currently trades at ~5x.
- ServiceTitan Initial Public Offering (December 2024): $9B opening valuation on $700M trailing-twelve-month Annual Recurring Revenue. 13x revenue multiple at issuance.

Sherpa Pros IPO scenario at $200M-$300M Annual Recurring Revenue at 8-12x multiple = $1.6B-$3.6B opening valuation. This is the upper-bound founder outcome.

**Why Special Purpose Acquisition Company (SPAC) is not attractive.** Post-2022 SPAC reset has cratered SPAC pricing and credibility. Sherpa Pros should not pursue a SPAC merger unless conditions reverse dramatically.

**What Initial Public Offering requires that strategic acquisition does not.**
- Sarbanes-Oxley Section 404 compliance and internal controls audit (12-18 months pre-Initial Public Offering).
- Public-company-grade Financial Planning and Analysis function (Chief Financial Officer's team scales 3x).
- Investor Relations function (new department, ~6 headcount).
- Quarterly earnings cadence and Securities and Exchange Commission reporting (10-Q, 10-K, 8-K).
- Director and officer insurance scaled to public-company tier.
- Long-term incentive plan re-architecture (Restricted Stock Units replace stock options for new grants).

## 2. Pre-engagement playbook (Phase 4 Year 2 → Phase 5 Year 1)

### 2.1 Year 1 (Phase 4 Year 2, Months 24-36): warm relationships, no formal process

**Goal.** By Series C close, every plausible strategic acquirer's corporate development team knows Sherpa Pros, has met Phyrom and the Chief Operating Officer, has reviewed the data room at a high level, and considers Sherpa Pros a known quantity.

**Quarterly cadence.**
- Q1 (Phase 4 M24-27): Phyrom + Chief Financial Officer (Phase 4 Month 6 hire) introduce Sherpa Pros to corporate development at Home Depot, Lowe's, Procore, ServiceTitan, Travelers, Chubb. Informal coffee, no NDA, no formal pitch — just relationship building.
- Q2 (M27-30): Send a quarterly business update (1-page memo, 6-7 metrics, no projections) to each corporate development relationship. Target: each acquirer knows our Annual Recurring Revenue trajectory without us having to pitch them again.
- Q3 (M30-33): Series C announcement triggers a press cycle that puts Sherpa Pros on every acquirer's radar at the same moment. Use the press cycle as the natural follow-up moment with each corporate development team.
- Q4 (M33-36): If any acquirer has expressed inbound interest, route through outside Mergers and Acquisitions counsel (do not respond directly). Document each interaction in a confidential corporate development tracker (board-only access).

### 2.2 Year 2 (Phase 5 Year 1, Months 36-48): formal banker engagement only if path becomes priority

**Trigger to engage a banker.** Either (a) a strategic acquirer makes a credible unsolicited offer above $500M, (b) the board decides exit is the better-of-two-worlds vs Initial Public Offering path, or (c) Series C financial performance accelerates faster than projected and acquirers compete for the asset.

**Banker shortlist.** Goldman Sachs (large-cap, full process), Morgan Stanley (technology-vertical specialist), J.P. Morgan (broadest acquirer network), Qatalyst Partners (boutique, technology Mergers and Acquisitions specialist, founded by Frank Quattrone), Lazard Middle Market (boutique). Recommend dual-track: one bulge-bracket banker + one boutique for redundancy.

**Process design.** 90-day "limited auction" with 4-6 strategic bidders + 2-3 financial bidders (private equity for take-private alternative). Founder + Chief Financial Officer present management; banker handles bid management and pricing tension.

### 2.3 Defensive posture

**What we never do.** We never accept a meeting where the acquirer asks "what would it take" without us controlling the process. We never share customer-level Profit and Loss data without an NDA + a board-approved process. We never let any single acquirer relationship advance without a comparable pacing on at least one alternative.

**What we always do.** Document every acquirer conversation in the corporate development tracker. Maintain board-level optionality on every decision. Keep the Initial Public Offering path real — even if we sell, the threat of Initial Public Offering disciplines the price.

## 3. Decision framework (when does the board choose strategic acquisition over Initial Public Offering)

The board chooses strategic acquisition over Initial Public Offering when ALL three of the following are true:

1. **Strategic premium exceeds public-market multiple by >25%.** If Home Depot offers $1.0B and Initial Public Offering scenario at the same Annual Recurring Revenue prices at $750M, strategic acquisition wins.
2. **Founder + executive bench are willing to operate inside the acquirer for the earn-out period.** Typical earn-out is 24-48 months. If executive team will leave on day one, strategic acquisition value collapses.
3. **Acquirer's strategic logic is stronger than Sherpa's standalone compounding logic.** If Sherpa standalone is on track to $500M Annual Recurring Revenue in 3 years, the strategic acquisition has to clear a high bar to be worth it. If Sherpa standalone is plateauing, the bar drops.

**Phyrom's stated preference (current state, may evolve).** Founder prefers independent compounding through Initial Public Offering, with strategic acquisition as a backstop. The Series C round is structured to fund the Initial Public Offering path; strategic acquisition is opportunistic, not the plan.

## 4. Board reporting cadence

- Quarterly: Corporate development tracker review at every board meeting beginning Phase 4 Month 24.
- Annually: Full strategic-options review including comparable-transaction update and acquirer-relationship status.
- Event-driven: Any unsolicited offer above $300M triggers an emergency board meeting within 5 business days.

## 5. Open questions to resolve in Phase 4

1. **Outside Mergers and Acquisitions counsel selection.** Recommend Wilson Sonsini Goodrich and Rosati or Cooley LLP for technology Mergers and Acquisitions, retained ahead of need (Phase 4 Month 18).
2. **Director of Corporate Development.** Should Sherpa hire this role in Phase 4 Year 2 (Month 24) to professionalize corporate development relationships, or wait until Phase 5 when Mergers and Acquisitions activity intensifies? Recommend Phase 5 Year 1 unless inbound activity demands earlier.
3. **Founder lock-up structure.** What earn-out terms is Phyrom willing to accept (typical: 50% cash at close, 50% over 36-month earn-out)? Worth establishing the founder's position before any acquirer asks.
4. **Employee retention pool.** What size retention pool does Sherpa carry into a Mergers and Acquisitions transaction (typical: 5-8% of deal value reserved for key employee retention)? Establish in Phase 4 Year 1 alongside Series C cap-table planning.
