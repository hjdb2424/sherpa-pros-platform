Canned Q&A — 7 objections + answers

Q1: Why hasn't Angi done this? A: Angi is a public lead-gen company that just restructured. Their entire revenue engine is per-lead pricing — pivoting to a marketplace + materials + code intelligence model is a 3-year ground-up rebuild, and they don't have a working-GC founder to lead it. Their operator culture is consumer-marketplace, not contractor-marketplace. They've explored licensed-trade vertical multiple times internally, killed it twice. Realistically they'll buy us before they build it. We've modeled them as a Series C strategic-check candidate, not a competitor.

Q2: What about Procore? A: Procore is an enterprise SaaS company that sells project management software to GCs and trade companies. They have zero marketplace exposure and zero materials exposure. We are additive, not competitive. Procore Ventures is on our Series C strategic-check list. The closest thing they've tried in marketplace is a connections directory, which is a phone book, not a transactional layer. They're a $7B public co built around enterprise contracts — different DNA, different shape.

Q3: What's defensible? What's the moat? A: Three moats stacked. (1) Founder profile — working GC with a 10-year distributor relationship and a 3-year codebase. That intersection is rare. (2) Code Intelligence Layer — the data we generate validating every quote against NEC, IRC, and state codes compounds with every job. By Series B we have 50,000+ live validations no one else has. (3) Distributor partnerships — FW Webb signed because Phyrom asked. The next FW Webb deal compounds the first. 24-month head start on distributor LOIs is hard to catch. None of these are fast to copy. All three reinforce each other.

Q4: FW Webb is a single point of failure. What if they walk? A: Three layers of mitigation. (1) Sherpa Materials engine is distributor-agnostic by design — it routes through any distributor with an inventory API. FW Webb is the first integration, not the only one. (2) Hub #2 is already in conversation with a second distributor (regional plumbing/HVAC supply with $400M annual revenue). (3) Even if FW Webb walked tomorrow, the marketplace + dispatch + code intelligence layers continue running on Phase 1 take rate. We'd lose the materials margin rail temporarily — 4-7% of blended take. Inconvenient, not fatal. And FW Webb has no incentive to walk: we're giving them a forecasting layer they can't build alone.

Q5: How do you compete with Home Depot Pro? A: Home Depot Pro is materials-only. They've tried labor matching twice — HomeServices.com (sold 2018 at a loss) and Iris (failure 2020). Their distribution and retail DNA is the wrong shape for a licensed marketplace. They sell to a homeowner walking through a store. We sell to a verified pro running a multi-trade job. Different customer, different unit economics. Likely Series C strategic-check candidate. They're more useful as an acquirer or partner than as a competitor.

Q6: The trade economy is fragmented. How do you crack it? A: That's exactly why this works. The same fragmentation that scared off lead-gen platforms — different state licensing, different code books, different rebate programs, different distributor relationships per region — is the reason no national pure-software player can win this. We crack it the way Toast cracked restaurants and ServiceTitan cracked HVAC: regional density first, code intelligence per state, distributor partnerships per region, then connect the regions through one brand and one payments rail. Phase 1 is Northern Triangle (NH/MA/ME). Phase 2 is 4 metros. Phase 3 is regional. Phase 4 is national + international. Fragmentation is the moat — for whoever masters it first.

Q7: Why now? Why isn't this 2024 or 2028? A: 2024 was too early — the AI was not code-literate yet. A code-aware quote validator that reads NEC and IRC in real time was a research paper in 2022. 2028 is too late — the licensed-marketplace quadrant is empty right now, but somebody else fills it inside 24 months. Also: post-COVID labor shortage is structural, materials inflation has rewired distribution, and Mass Save / IRA / EV-charger rebate dollars are hitting the street in 2026. Four tailwinds converging in one 12-month window is rare. We're at the exact intersection. The Series A is the window — not the deck.
