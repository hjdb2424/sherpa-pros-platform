The basics

Q1. Who can invest in Sherpa Pros on Wefunder?

Almost any U.S. resident 18 or older can invest. You do not need to be an accredited investor (a high-income or high-net-worth investor under SEC rules). This raise is offered under SEC Regulation Crowdfunding (Reg CF), which lets everyday people invest in private startups for as little as $100.

There are SEC-set caps on how much non-accredited investors can put into Reg CF deals across all platforms in a 12-month period. The cap depends on your annual income and net worth. Wefunder will calculate and enforce the cap during checkout, so you do not need to do that math yourself.

If you are an accredited investor (generally, an individual with $200K+ income or $1M+ net worth excluding primary residence — see the SEC definition for current thresholds), there is no Reg CF investment cap for you.

International investors: case-by-case. Wefunder will check during checkout.

Q2. What does my investment actually buy me?

A SAFE. That stands for Simple Agreement for Future Equity. It is a contract that gives you the right to convert your investment into shares of Sherpa Pros stock later, when one of these things happens:

  1. The company raises money from venture capitalists or other investors at a "priced round" (an investment that sets a per-share price for Sherpa Pros stock). Your SAFE converts into shares at that price, with the discount and valuation cap explained below.
  2. The company is acquired by another company. Your SAFE pays out cash based on the acquisition price.
  3. The company has an Initial Public Offering (IPO) — much later, if ever.

In plain English: a SAFE is a placeholder for shares. You hand the company money today. You do not get shares today. You get shares (or cash) later when there's a triggering event.

Q3. What is the valuation cap, and why does it matter?

The valuation cap is the maximum company value at which your SAFE will convert into shares. Lower cap = better for you (you get more shares for your money).

Sherpa Pros is offering a SAFE at a $5M–$8M valuation cap (final number will be set by the founder with attorney review before public launch — see Q23). At a $6M cap, every $1,000 you invest buys roughly 1/6,000th of the company at conversion (assuming the company raises a future priced round at any value above $6M).

Worked example. You invest $1,000 at a $6M cap. Six months later, Sherpa Pros raises a priced round at a $24M valuation. Your $1,000 converts as if you had paid the $6M-cap price, not the $24M price. You get 4× more shares than the new investors paying $24M.

Q4. What is the discount?

The SAFE includes a 20% discount in addition to the valuation cap. If the future priced round is below the cap, the discount kicks in instead — you pay 80% of what new investors pay per share.

You always get the better of the two (cap or discount), so you are protected on both ends.

Q5. What is "MFN" and does this SAFE have it?

MFN stands for Most Favored Nation. It means: if Sherpa Pros later issues a SAFE to another investor on better terms (lower cap, bigger discount, more protective rights), your SAFE automatically upgrades to those better terms. You are not penalized for investing early.

Yes, this Wefunder SAFE includes MFN. Confirmed in the SAFE document attached to the offering page.

Q6. When and how do I get my money back?

Three paths:

  1. Next priced equity round. When Sherpa Pros raises from a venture capital firm at a per-share price, your SAFE converts to shares (at the better of the cap or discount). You then own actual stock. You get money back when (a) the company is acquired, (b) the company IPOs, or (c) you sell the shares on a secondary market — which is restricted and rare for early-stage private stock.
  2. Acquisition. If Sherpa Pros is bought by another company before a priced round, your SAFE pays out cash according to the SAFE's terms. This is faster but not guaranteed to happen.
  3. Company fails. If Sherpa Pros runs out of money and shuts down, you lose your investment. SAFEs are unsecured. There is no guaranteed payout.

There is no scheduled "payback date." This is not a loan. This is not a bond. There is no interest. Plan to lock up your money for 5–10 years minimum — and to lose it entirely if the company fails.

Comparing it to other things

Q12. How is this different from Kickstarter or GoFundMe?

Kickstarter and GoFundMe are donations or rewards-based crowdfunding. You give money to back a project. You get a t-shirt, a gadget, or a thank-you. You do not own anything.

Wefunder Reg CF is equity crowdfunding. You give money in exchange for the right to a future ownership stake in the company (the SAFE). If the company succeeds, you get a return. If it fails, you lose the money. You own a piece of the business.

Q13. Can I lose all my money?

Yes. You can lose every dollar you invest. This is real and the SEC requires us to say it clearly.

Most early-stage startups fail. Industry data on Reg CF outcomes is still maturing, but a meaningful portion of community-round companies do not survive 5 years. Sherpa Pros could fail — see the Risks section of the offering page (and Section 11 of the GTM design spec for the founder's own risk register).

Only invest money you can fully afford to lose. Do not invest money you need for rent, groceries, retirement near-term, your kids' tuition, or an emergency fund. A common rule of thumb: do not put more than 1–5% of your investable assets into early-stage private startups.

Q16. Tax treatment — what do I need to know?

You will receive a Schedule K-1 (or equivalent) annually from Sherpa Pros' SAFE-holding entity if structured through a Wefunder special-purpose vehicle (SPV). For most Wefunder Reg CF deals investors receive a K-1 partnership tax form.

Practical points:

  • Until your SAFE converts to shares (or pays out via acquisition), there is typically no taxable event for you year-over-year. The K-1 may show $0 in income.
  • When the SAFE converts and shares are eventually sold (via acquisition or secondary), gains may be taxed as long-term or short-term capital gains depending on holding period.
  • Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS, Section 1202) may apply if the SAFE converts to qualifying stock and is held 5+ years — potentially up to 100% federal capital gains exclusion on up to $10M. Eligibility is technical and not guaranteed. Talk to your CPA.
  • Losses on a failed startup investment may be deductible as a capital loss or, in some cases, ordinary loss under Section 1244. Talk to your CPA.

This is not tax advice. Talk to your CPA before investing if tax treatment matters to your decision.

Q18. What happens to my SAFE if Sherpa Pros raises money from a "priced round" at a low valuation?

The 20% discount kicks in. You pay 80% of the new investor per-share price.

If the priced round is below the SAFE's cap (the company is valued lower than expected), the discount controls and protects you. If the priced round is above the cap (the company is valued higher than expected), the cap controls and protects you — you get the lower of the two effective prices, meaning more shares per dollar.

You always get the better of the two protections.

Q19. What happens to my SAFE if Sherpa Pros gets acquired before a priced round?

The SAFE pays out cash based on the SAFE's "Liquidity Event" terms. In short: you get the greater of (a) your money back, or (b) the share-equivalent value at the cap.

Worked example. You invest $1,000 at a $6M cap. Eighteen months later, Sherpa Pros is acquired for $30M. The cap-equivalent share entitles you to roughly $5,000 ($1,000 × $30M ÷ $6M). You get $5,000.

If the acquisition is for less than the cap (a fire sale), you get your $1,000 back (assuming the buyer can pay; senior debt and preferred shares are paid first, which is rare for early-stage SAFE-only cap tables but possible).

Q20. What happens to my SAFE if Sherpa Pros fails?

You lose your investment. SAFEs sit at the bottom of the priority stack in a wind-down — after employees, vendors, debt holders, and any preferred equity holders. In a typical small-startup wind-down there is no money left for SAFE holders.

This is the risk you accept by investing in a Reg CF round.

What changed this quarter (the new vertical-integration story)

Q31. What is the difference between Sherpa Pros and Angi or Thumbtack?

Angi and Thumbtack are labor-only lead-gen platforms. They sell a contractor the chance to bid on a homeowner job — and then the contractor still has to handle everything else themselves: ordering the materials, picking them up, coordinating any other trades on the project, and managing the schedule.

Sherpa Pros is a vertically integrated trade orchestration platform. That is a long phrase for a simple idea: we handle four things on every job, not one.

  1. Labor — like Angi and Thumbtack, we match a homeowner with a licensed pro. Unlike them, we charge a take rate when the job actually completes (not a lead fee for the chance to bid).
  2. Materials — through Sherpa Materials, the platform orders all the materials for the job from the major retail catalogs and gets them delivered to the jobsite the same day through Uber Direct. Angi and Thumbtack do not do this at all.
  3. Delivery — same-day delivery is built in. The pro does not lose half a day driving to the supply house. Angi and Thumbtack do not do this either.
  4. Coordination — through Sherpa Dispatch, the platform sequences every trade on a multi-trade project (a kitchen renovation can involve 6+ trades) on one timeline, so the project finishes in 5 weeks instead of 11. Angi and Thumbtack do not touch this layer at all.

The plain-English version: Angi and Thumbtack compete in one lane (labor matching). Sherpa Pros operates in four lanes (labor + materials + delivery + coordination). The competitive set for Sherpa Pros is no longer the labor-marketplace category — it is the entire trade-supply-chain stack (companies like FW Webb, Grainger, Procore, and the same-day delivery services). Nobody is putting all four of those lanes together for the residential and small-commercial trade. That is the wedge.

For a longer explainer, see Section 4.5 and Section 4.6 of the Wefunder page.

Q32. How do you make money?

We make money on every job in four stacked ways:

  1. Labor commission. When a pro completes a job, we take a percentage of the labor cost. The rate depends on the pro tier — 5 percent forever for our Founding Pros, 8 percent for top-rated Gold-tier pros, 12 percent for Silver and Bronze, 18 percent for Sherpa Flex (our no-LLC entry tier, which includes $1 million per-project liability insurance).
  2. Materials coordination fee. When the pro orders the job's materials through Sherpa Materials, we add an 8 to 12 percent coordination fee on top of the wholesale materials cost. This stacks on top of the labor commission — it is a separate revenue line.
  3. Pro subscription. Pros pay $49 per month for the standard subscription tier (free during beta).
  4. Property Manager tier and Success Manager retainer. Property managers pay $4 per unit per month at entry, dropping to $1.50 per unit per month at scale (5,000+ units). White-glove "Sherpa Manager" accounts pay an additional retainer for a real human running the account.

The blended take rate per job — all four revenue lines combined — works out to roughly 18 to 25 percent of the all-in job cost (labor + materials). That is a different category of marketplace than Angi or Thumbtack, where the take rate ceiling is structurally 10 to 15 percent because labor is the only revenue line.

This is also why Sherpa Materials matters so much for the company's path to profitability. Materials revenue has near-zero customer acquisition cost — every job we already booked is a materials-coordination opportunity at no extra acquisition spend. The materials line shortens the runway calculus for the same Phase 0 ask.

Q33. What does "multi-trade coordination" mean?

The plain-English answer: a kitchen renovation looks simple from the outside, but inside it is six or more separate trades that all have to show up in the right order.

Take a typical kitchen remodel:

  1. Demolition rips out the old cabinets, countertops, and flooring.
  2. Electrical has to rough-in new outlets, the dishwasher line, the range hood, and recessed lighting — but only after demo and before drywall.
  3. Plumbing has to rough-in the new sink, dishwasher, and refrigerator water line — same window as electrical.
  4. Drywall patches and finishes — only after electrical and plumbing are inspected.
  5. Paint has to come in after drywall is done — but before cabinets so paint splatter isn't an issue.
  6. Tile for the backsplash — after paint, before cabinets.
  7. Cabinetry install — after paint and backsplash tile.
  8. Finish carpentry for trim, crown molding, and any built-ins — last.

If even one of those trades shows up on the wrong day — say plumbing arrives before electrical is done — the whole project slips by a week or more. Today, most contractors juggle that with a paper notebook, a spreadsheet, a group text, and a lot of swearing. An 11-week kitchen renovation that runs 3 weeks late (so 14 weeks total) is the industry norm.

Sherpa Dispatch puts every one of those trades on one timeline, owns the handoff between them, and warns everyone in the chain before a delay becomes a slip. A real-world example from our beta: that same 11-week kitchen renovation finishes in 5 weeks with Sherpa Dispatch — because the trades are sequenced correctly the first time. Faster project completion means happier homeowners, happier pros (who get paid sooner), and happier property managers (whose unit comes back online for rent sooner).

Multi-trade coordination is the layer enterprise general contractors pay $1,000+ per user per month for software like Procore to handle. We bring it down to the residential and small-commercial scale and bake it into the Sherpa Pros platform every customer already uses. Nobody else in the licensed-trade marketplace category does this.

International expansion is a Phase 4 commitment, not a Phase 1 or Phase 2 promise. We are sharing the staircase so investors understand the long-term opportunity, not so anyone treats it as a near-term milestone.
